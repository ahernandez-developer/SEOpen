How to Start a Sourdough Starter

By Maya Lindqvist, Head Baker at Vestra Bakery

Published April 1, 2026 · Updated April 10, 2026

A sourdough starter is a small, living community of wild yeast and friendly bacteria that you keep on your kitchen counter. Once it is alive and active, it can raise bread loaves for the rest of your life. This guide walks a complete beginner through the craft, from the very first mix to the first loaf you bake with it. The work is gentle. The ingredients are familiar. The rewards are real.

What is a sourdough starter?

A sourdough starter is flour and water, mixed and left to ferment. The flour naturally carries wild yeasts and bacteria. When you add water, those microbes wake up, begin to eat the sugars in the flour, and produce the bubbles and tangy flavour that classic sourdough is known for. You feed the starter fresh flour and water each day, the community grows, and within a week or two the mixture is strong enough to leaven a loaf on its own.

Unlike commercial baker's yeast, which is a single strain cultivated in a laboratory, a starter is a miniature ecosystem. Every starter is shaped by the flour it is fed, the water you use, and the microclimate of your kitchen. Two starters raised side by side in different homes will taste subtly different, and that is part of the craft.

Materials you will need

You probably have most of this in your kitchen already. If not, a short trip to the grocery store will cover everything.

  • A clean glass jar with a loose-fitting lid — a mason jar works beautifully.
  • A wooden or silicone spoon — avoid reactive metals.
  • A kitchen scale — volume measurements will drift, weight will not.
  • Whole-grain rye or whole-wheat flour — these kick-start fermentation quickly.
  • Unbleached bread flour or all-purpose flour — for ongoing feedings.
  • Filtered or spring water — chlorinated tap water can slow the microbes.
  • A rubber band or strip of tape — handy for marking the starter's rise level.

Step-by-step instructions

The routine below follows a daily rhythm. Pick a time of day that fits your schedule and try to stay close to it — your starter likes a predictable kitchen.

  1. On day one, mix equal parts whole-grain flour and water in your clean jar, stir until smooth, and loosely cover it. Leave it on the counter at room temperature.
  2. On day two, you may see a few bubbles or nothing at all. Either is fine. Discard half of the mixture, then feed the rest with fresh flour and water, stir, cover, and wait.
  3. On days three through six, repeat the discard and feed each day. You should see the starter rise and fall between feedings, and a pleasantly sour smell should begin to develop.
  4. By day seven or eight, the starter should roughly double in size within a few hours of being fed. When it does, it is ready to raise a loaf of bread.
  5. Once you are baking regularly, keep the starter in the refrigerator and feed it weekly. Bring it back to room temperature and give it a feed before each baking day.

Comparing flour choices

Different flours produce different starter personalities. The table below is a loose orientation; experiment and see what your kitchen prefers.

Flour type Typical behaviour Best for
Whole-grain rye Fast and pungent Getting a new starter going quickly
Whole-wheat Lively and slightly sweet Rustic country loaves
Bread flour Gentle and predictable Daily feedings and most baking
All-purpose flour Mild and approachable Beginners who want a forgiving starter

What a good routine looks like

A healthy starter is a boring starter — and that is a compliment. It should rise and fall on a predictable rhythm, smell pleasantly tangy, and pass the float test when you drop a small spoonful into water. If your starter is sluggish, warm the kitchen a little, switch to a more active flour for a few feedings, and be patient. The microbes reward a steady hand.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I really need to discard half of my starter each day?

Yes, at least in the early days. Discarding keeps the acidity in check and gives the microbes enough fresh food to thrive. Once your starter is strong, you can reduce the discard by using smaller feeding ratios.

Can I use tap water?

In most places, yes, but heavily chlorinated water can slow the microbes down. If your starter seems sluggish, try leaving a pitcher of tap water uncovered overnight so the chlorine can dissipate, or switch to filtered or spring water.

My starter smells like acetone. Is it ruined?

Almost certainly not. A solvent-like smell usually means the starter is hungry. Give it a fresh feed, wait a few hours, and the aroma should return to a gentle tang.

How do I know when my starter is strong enough to bake with?

When it reliably roughly doubles within a few hours of being fed, smells pleasantly sour, and floats a small spoonful on top of a glass of water, it is ready for its first loaf.
